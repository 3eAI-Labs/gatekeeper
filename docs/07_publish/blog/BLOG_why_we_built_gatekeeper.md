# Why we built Gatekeeper

*2026-04-25 · 3eAI Labs Ltd · Posted under the Apache 2.0 community-tier release of v0.1.1*

The origin story for most infrastructure software is some variant of "we needed it and nothing existed". Gatekeeper is no exception, but the version of "we needed it and nothing existed" that pushed us to build it is specific enough that it's worth telling honestly. This is not the polished version that sits in a pitch deck. This is what actually happened.

## The conversation that started it

Late 2025, a senior engineer at a Turkish telco was telling us about an AI assistant they had shipped to their customer-care platform. The assistant was good — measurably reducing average handle time, getting positive customer-satisfaction scores, paying back its development cost faster than anyone had projected. It was also, by his own description, "operating on hope and prayers" from a governance perspective.

He listed the things that worried him in roughly the order they had come to bother him. The OpenAI bill had grown faster than the team had budgeted for, and there was no per-team attribution — when the finance director asked which business line owned the cost, nobody could answer with confidence. A junior engineer on a different team had pasted a customer's transcript directly into the assistant for testing, and the customer's MSISDN, name, and partial address had gone to OpenAI before anyone realised. The team wanted to evaluate Anthropic's Claude as a possible alternative for some use cases, but the only way to do so safely was to write a parallel codepath and shadow-test manually — which nobody had time to do, so they were considering just switching cold and seeing what broke.

None of these problems were exotic. Every one of them was the kind of thing you would expect a mature platform team to handle through the normal infrastructure they already operated. The reason this team — a competent team, by every measure — was operating on hope and prayers was that the existing tools did not fit. The API gateway they ran (APISIX, as it happens, which is partly why this story matters here) was great at HTTP routing and authentication; it had no native concept of "AI traffic" or "token quota" or "per-prompt PII scanning". The observability stack saw bytes in and bytes out; it had no idea that the bytes contained a customer's national ID. The change-management process worked beautifully for backend service deploys; it had nothing to say about swapping the LLM that an existing service called.

We started looking around at what other people were building for this. There were, broadly, three categories of thing.

The first category was prompt management products — tools for versioning prompts, doing A/B tests on prompt templates, providing a UI for non-technical users to edit prompts. These solved a real problem but not the one our telco friend was describing. He did not need to manage prompts; he needed to know what was in them and what they cost.

The second category was AI observability products — tools that logged prompts and completions, computed cost analytics after the fact, generated dashboards. These were closer to the problem but architecturally upside-down: they observed the AI traffic from the side, after it had already happened, when what our telco friend needed was something *in front of* the traffic that could enforce policy in real time and prevent the bad things from happening in the first place.

The third category was full-blown AI-platform-in-a-box products that wanted to replace large parts of the existing infrastructure with their own gateway, their own auth model, their own observability. These solved the governance problem by sidestepping it — they assumed you had no existing gateway, no existing auth, no existing audit pipeline. For a greenfield deployment that might have been fine. For a telco with thirty years of accumulated infrastructure, it was a non-starter.

What was missing was something simple. A plugin for the gateway the team already operated, with the governance primitives that were missing — token quotas, multi-provider routing with circuit breakers, PII scanning with regex and NER, structural shadow diff for canary deployments, an audit trail that landed in the database the team already operated. Apache 2.0 so the security review could read every line. Self-hosted because the data sovereignty conversation was non-negotiable. Persona-aware because a CISO and a CFO and a DPO all want different things from the same product and pretending otherwise produces marketing soup that resonates with no one.

So we built it.

## The architectural choices that mattered

A few decisions we made early turned out to be more consequential than we anticipated, and they are worth explaining because they shape what the product is.

**The gateway is APISIX, specifically.** We considered Kong, Tyk, Envoy, and a custom proxy. APISIX won because the OpenResty plugin model is genuinely well-suited to the per-request work that AI governance needs — Lua running inside Nginx workers is fast enough for the hot path and expressive enough for non-trivial logic, and APISIX's plugin lifecycle hooks (`access`, `body_filter`, `header_filter`, `log`) line up cleanly with the phases of an AI request. The Apache 2.0 license matters for the open-core model we adopted. The CNCF presence and the active community matter for long-term maintainability. None of these are uniquely true of APISIX, but the combination is hard to beat. Kong was the closest second; we have not ruled out shipping a Kong-equivalent eventually if the operator demand is there.

**The split between Lua and Java was deliberate, not lazy.** The Lua plugins handle the per-request hot path; the Java sidecar handles the heavy work. We had several arguments about this internally — variants like "do everything in Lua and skip the sidecar" or "do everything in the sidecar and make the gateway a thin proxy" both got serious consideration. The split won because Lua is excellent at the per-request orchestration and miserable at things like accurate tokenisation against an OpenAI-compatible BPE encoding or running a multilingual NER model through ONNX Runtime; meanwhile, Java is excellent at those things and would be miserable as the per-request hot path inside a high-throughput gateway. Forcing one onto the other compromises both. Splitting them — with HTTP/JSON over loopback as the bridge — keeps each in its zone.

**The transport between Lua and the sidecar took a few iterations to get right.** Our original architecture (ADR-003, in the project's decision register) called for gRPC over Unix Domain Sockets — a low-latency, kernel-level-protected channel that fit the latency-sensitivity of the request hot path. After several attempts to make a Lua gRPC client work robustly inside OpenResty, we reversed the decision and switched to HTTP/JSON over loopback TCP. The reasons were prosaic: Lua does not have a maintained gRPC client, the alternatives are a custom FFI binding (multi-week effort plus ongoing maintenance) or no client at all; the latency difference between UDS gRPC (~0.1ms) and loopback TCP HTTP/JSON (~1-2ms) is dominated by the LLM provider call (~50ms to several seconds) by orders of magnitude and is therefore not material; and HTTP/JSON is debuggable with `curl` while gRPC requires `grpcurl` plus the proto file. The boring option was the right option. We documented this in ADR-008 and we have not regretted it once.

**The audit pipeline ended up being the most-discussed design choice in the project.** The original plan was that Lua would emit audit events and the sidecar would consume them. The interesting question was *how*. The first proposal was a third HTTP bridge — `POST /v1/audit/event` — and an early variant considered a hybrid that wrote both to the HTTP bridge (for fast-path persistence) and to a Redis buffer (for catch-up if the bridge was down). When we put the hybrid on the table, the project owner asked a question that crystallised the design problem: "Why two paths?" The two-path design did not actually buy any additional safety — it just meant two failure modes to reason about, two pipelines to monitor, and zero added durability. The simpler design — Lua pushes to Redis, sidecar polls Redis with LPOP on a scheduled tick — won, and it is genuinely better. ADR-009 documents the choice. The lesson for us was that the "neden iki path?" instinct is one we should apply more often.

## The thing we learned the hard way

We shipped a v1.0 baseline on 2026-04-08, with all the documents that go with a Sentinel-SDLC-flavoured release: high-level design, low-level design, error code registry, architecture decision records, the works. Over the seventeen days that followed, we shipped real feature work — the NER bridge, the structural shadow diff, the real token counter, the HTTP transport that replaced the originally-planned UDS gRPC. The spec did not catch up.

We caught the drift because we ran a deliberately adversarial spec review on 2026-04-25, comparing the running code to the documentation. The review surfaced fifteen findings, six of them critical, including the audit pipeline that the documentation claimed was working but in fact had no consumer for its events. The Lua side was correctly emitting events to Redis; the Java side never read them; the audit table received no inserts on a fresh deployment.

This was uncomfortable. We had a "Must"-priority business rule (BR-SH-015) that we believed was implemented and was not. We had release notes that claimed PCI-DSS compliance in language that, on re-reading, would not have survived a regulator's first question. We had architecture decisions documented that no longer matched the running code. The instinct in this situation is to fix it quietly and pretend it never happened, but that instinct is wrong: the value of the documentation is precisely that it can be trusted, and the way to make it trusted is to be transparent about the times it was not.

So we wrote it down. The PHASE_REVIEW_2026-04-25 document is the full audit trail, kept frozen as an input artefact. The v1.1 spec freeze (commit `a63986f`) reconciled every spec document to the running reality. The v1.1.1 freeze closed the audit-pipeline gap properly with ADR-009. The v1.1.2 freeze closed the Flyway bootstrap gap. The compliance framing was reworked to capability statements. A `GUIDELINES_MANIFEST.yaml` `phase_gates.require_human_signature` rule was added to make silent acceptance of Phase 6 artefacts a process violation rather than just a missed checkbox. A `HUMAN_SIGN_OFF_v0.1.0.md` document was added to the release process — every release tag now requires an explicit signature from the maintainer before the tag can be created.

We are documenting this here because we think the lessons matter for any team building governance infrastructure of their own. **Spec drift is the default outcome of small teams shipping fast**, and the solution is not "be more disciplined" — that solution does not survive contact with shipping pressure. The solution is to make spec drift cheap to detect and mandatory to close, through automated processes that do not rely on a human catching it in code review. **The audit pipeline is the most under-attended part of every governance system we have seen, including the one we built**, and it deserves integration tests that verify end-to-end persistence rather than unit tests that verify the two ends separately and miss the gap between them. **Compliance framing is a discipline, not a one-time decision**, and capability statements are durable in a way that certification claims are not.

## Where we are now

The v0.1.1 release, which is what is currently on GitHub, is the first version where everything claimed in the documentation is actually shipped in the code. Both v0.1 critical gaps that the adversarial review surfaced are closed. The four remaining minor gaps are listed by name in the [release notes](../../06_review/RELEASE_NOTES_v0.1.0_2026-04-25.md) — the `ariactl` CLI is deferred to v0.2, the sidecar prompt-analysis stubs are deferred to v0.3 enterprise CISO tier, the reversible tokenisation is deferred to v0.2, and the token role-overhead semantics is open as a v0.2 ADR.

The community tier — three Lua plugins under Apache 2.0 plus the sidecar engine — is enough to ship into production today for the cost-control, PII-masking, and progressive-delivery use cases that motivated the project. The enterprise tiers are organised by buyer persona rather than by feature flag — security buyer (CISO) gets the prompt-injection corpus and content moderation; privacy buyer (DPO) gets the bundled multilingual NER models and DLP-style outbound mask; finance buyer (CFO) gets the advanced billing reconciliation and per-team chargeback. The persona model means the community tier is not a crippled demo of the enterprise tier; it is a complete product with its own ceiling, and organisations that hit that ceiling because of a specific persona-driven need can adopt the matching tier without taking on the full enterprise scope.

We would rather ship a smaller product that an operator can run with confidence than a larger product that an operator has to take on faith. Everything we have done so far is built on that preference, and we expect to keep building on it.

## What's next

The v0.2 work in flight: the `ariactl` CLI as a thin Go binary wrapping the existing canary control endpoints, reversible AES-256-backed tokenisation in the mask plugin, the Karar B token role-overhead ADR with the OpenAI standard locked in, a JaCoCo coverage gate plus a SAST re-run against the post-NER head as part of the CI pipeline, and the consolidation of the V001..V003 migration files (currently vendored in two locations for v0.1 deployment-flexibility reasons) into the sidecar repository as the single source of truth.

The v0.3 enterprise CISO tier work: the vector-similarity prompt-injection corpus and the content-moderation pipeline that will turn the currently-stubbed `PromptAnalyzer` and `ContentFilter` services into real implementations. The investment that justifies the enterprise tier is the corpus itself — continuously curated, tested against novel injection variants — and the integration with the threat-intelligence feeds that make it durably useful.

The v0.3 enterprise DPO tier work: bundled multilingual NER models for languages and clinical domains beyond the Turkish-BERT default, DLP-style outbound mask with policy-based redaction, and the WORM (write-once-read-many) audit-log packaging that some compliance frameworks specifically require.

If you are reading it because you are thinking about building governance infrastructure of your own, the [WHITE_PAPER](../product/WHITE_PAPER.md) is the long-form argument for why governance belongs at the gateway and the trade-offs we made along the way.
