# Persona-gated open core: an alternative to feature gating

*Why we organised the enterprise tiers by buyer rather than by feature flag, and what we believe about the economics of governance infrastructure*

*2026-04-25 · 3eAI Labs Ltd*

The conversations about open-core licensing tend to be conducted at one of two altitudes. At the strategic-marketing altitude, open core is a positioning choice — a way to talk about commitment to open source while preserving a commercial revenue stream. At the engineering altitude, open core is a packaging choice — what code goes in the public repo, what code goes in the private repo, where the line is drawn.

Neither of these altitudes addresses the question that we found ourselves wrestling with: *what should the enterprise tier actually be*? The default answer in the industry — gate features behind a paywall, the community version has the basics, the enterprise version has the advanced ones — has a structural failure mode that we wanted to avoid. This post explains the alternative we picked, why we picked it, and what we believe it implies about how governance infrastructure should be priced.

## The failure mode of feature gating

Most open-core models gate features. The community version of an observability product has basic dashboards; the enterprise version has SAML login, role-based access control, an audit log, alerting integrations beyond Slack, and longer retention. The community version of a database product is the storage engine; the enterprise version has clustering, backup tooling, monitoring integrations, and a UI. The community version of a security product has rule-based detection; the enterprise version has machine-learning detection, threat intelligence feeds, and incident-response integrations.

This pattern is fine in the abstract. The failure mode shows up over time and is structural enough that it is worth describing in detail.

The first symptom is **community-version atrophy**. Every quarter, the engineering team ships features. Every quarter, a fraction of those features are gated to the enterprise tier because they are "advanced" or "operational" or "scaled". The community version, which started as a complete-enough product, gradually becomes a demo of the enterprise version. After a year or two, the community version is something nobody actually deploys for real work — it is something you install briefly to see if you would want the enterprise version, and then you either pay or you go away. The community is not really a community at this point; it is a sales funnel.

The second symptom is **gating-decision drift**. The features that get gated are not the features that are objectively "advanced"; they are the features that the sales team believes can be sold. Over time, this creates a strange product where some objectively-basic capabilities live behind the paywall (because they were the easiest to demo as differentiating in a sales meeting) and some objectively-sophisticated capabilities are open (because they were already in the community version when the gating discipline was introduced and pulling them back would have caused public uproar). The product becomes incoherent — neither tier has a clean story about what it does and why.

The third symptom is **community-credibility erosion**. As the community version atrophies and the gating decisions drift, the public perception of the project shifts. The project is no longer "an open-source product with a paid tier"; it is "a paid product with an open-source teaser". The contributors who were attracted by the open-source story leave because they don't want to volunteer features that will end up in someone else's enterprise SKU. The early adopters who built infrastructure on the community version feel betrayed when capabilities they relied on get gated. The marketing material starts using the phrase "open source at our core" rather than "open source", which is a phrase that almost always means "less open source than you think".

We have watched this pattern play out in several adjacent product categories — observability, identity, security tooling, gateway products themselves — and we did not want to repeat it. The choice we made required articulating what the enterprise tier should *be* before we wrote any code that would belong to it.

## The alternative: persona-gated tiers

Instead of gating features, we organised the enterprise tiers around **buyer personas**. The community tier is a complete product. The enterprise tiers are layered on top, each addressing the deeper, more specialised needs of a specific kind of buyer.

The three personas we identified for Gatekeeper are the **Chief Information Security Officer**, the **Data Protection Officer**, and the **Chief Financial Officer / FinOps lead**. These are not arbitrary categories; they are the three roles in the buying organisation that ask materially different questions about AI governance.

The CISO is asking about prompt-side security and sustained protection against adversarial input. The questions are about vector-similarity injection scoring, the corpus of known injection patterns, the integration with threat-intelligence feeds, the content-moderation pipeline, the response to novel attack variants. The investment that justifies a paid tier here is the **continuously-curated injection corpus** — the kind of work that requires sustained engineering effort, regular updates to track novel attack patterns, and the integration relationships with threat-intelligence vendors that make a corpus durably useful. The corpus is a moat, not the code that uses it; the corpus is what we charge for.

The DPO is asking about data minimisation, multilingual NER, audit-log packaging for regulatory submission, and DLP-style outbound mask. The questions are about the bundled multilingual model artefacts (Turkish-BERT for Turkish citizens, Arabic models for MENA-region citizens, Persian, Russian, additional clinical-domain variants), the WORM (write-once-read-many) audit-log packaging that some compliance frameworks specifically require, and the integration with the data-protection compliance reporting infrastructure the organisation already operates. The investment that justifies a paid tier here is the **model bundling and the regulatory-export packaging** — the kind of work that requires keeping up with model updates, maintaining the conversion pipelines (HuggingFace to ONNX, license verification, model-card maintenance), and producing the evidence packages that compliance teams hand to regulators. The community tier ships the engine code under Apache 2.0; the model artefacts are operator-supplied or enterprise-bundled.

The CFO is asking about cost attribution, chargeback, forecasting, and the integration with the organisation's existing financial systems. The questions are about per-team cost rollup, per-business-line attribution, per-cost-center reconciliation, and the connection to the existing FP&A pipeline that the finance organisation already runs. The investment that justifies a paid tier here is the **billing-pipeline integration and the chargeback report generation** — the kind of work that requires understanding the customer's specific finance infrastructure, building the connectors to their financial systems, and maintaining the integration as their finance practice evolves. The community tier ships token quotas, dollar budgets, and threshold alerts; the enterprise tier ships the chargeback infrastructure that turns those primitives into a reportable line item in the finance system.

The thing to notice about all three of these tiers is that **none of them are gating community-tier features**. We did not take the audit pipeline and gate it behind the DPO tier; the audit pipeline is in the Apache 2.0 community tier. We did not take the multi-provider routing and gate it behind the CFO tier; the routing is in the community tier. We did not take the regex prompt-injection scan and gate it behind the CISO tier; the regex layer is in the community tier. The enterprise tiers add capabilities that *only exist because the enterprise tier exists* — capabilities whose long-term curation, integration burden, or specialised investment cannot be sustained inside a community-driven model.

This is the structural difference. With feature-gated open core, the enterprise tier is built by *taking away* from the community version. With persona-gated open core, the enterprise tier is built by *adding to* what the community version provides. The community tier is not a crippled demo; it is a complete product with its own ceiling.

## Why this works for governance infrastructure specifically

The persona-gated model would not work for every product category. It works for governance infrastructure because of three structural properties of the category.

The first property is that **governance is bought by named roles, not by anonymous developers**. A developer might pick up an open-source database without involving any other role in the organisation. A developer cannot pick up an AI-governance product without eventually engaging with the CISO, the DPO, the CFO, or some equivalent. The buyer-persona structure exists in the customer organisation regardless of how we organise our tiers; we are matching our SKU structure to the buying structure that already exists.

The second property is that **governance investments compound over time**. The CISO's injection corpus is more valuable in year three than year one because it has accumulated three years of attack patterns. The DPO's multilingual model bundle is more valuable as it covers more languages and more clinical domains. The CFO's chargeback integration is more valuable as it deepens the connection to the customer's finance systems. The community tier is the *baseline* that does not require ongoing curation; the enterprise tiers are the *investments* that require sustained engineering effort to keep current.

The third property is that **the buyers in each persona have different procurement patterns**. The CISO's procurement involves a security-review cycle, a threat-intelligence-vendor evaluation, and an integration with the organisation's incident-response platform. The DPO's procurement involves a data-protection-impact-assessment, a model-bias evaluation, and an integration with the compliance reporting infrastructure. The CFO's procurement involves a finance-system integration evaluation and a chargeback-model alignment. These three procurements happen in three different rooms with three different timelines and three different evaluation criteria. Trying to sell all three personas the same SKU produces a SKU that fits none of them well; matching SKUs to personas produces SKUs that fit each one cleanly.

## What we are explicitly not doing

We are not running Gatekeeper as a hosted service. The community tier and all three enterprise tiers are self-hosted; the operator runs them on their own infrastructure with their own data stores. This is a deliberate choice that follows from the data-sovereignty considerations that motivate the product in the first place — putting governance infrastructure into a hosted-by-vendor model would defeat much of the point. We do not rule out a managed offering in the long term, but for the foreseeable future the deployment shape is exclusively self-hosted and the documentation is written accordingly.

We are not selling per-seat or per-user licensing. The enterprise tiers are organisational subscriptions; the licensing is by deployment scope rather than by user count. Per-seat licensing would create perverse incentives for operators to artificially constrain access to the governance tooling, which is the opposite of what we want.

We are not selling the code. The Apache 2.0 community tier is the code; the enterprise tiers are subscriptions to the curated assets (injection corpus, bundled NER models, billing integrations) and the engineering support that goes with them. An enterprise-tier customer who wants to read the source of any specific capability can do so, including the capabilities they are paying for; the value of the enterprise tier is in the curation and the support, not in source-code privacy.

We are not building a community-edition that forces upgrades. There is no nag screen in the community tier. There is no time-limited trial that converts to paid. There is no "this feature is locked" dialog that shows up in the open-source build. The community tier is a complete product that operates indefinitely without any external dependencies; an organisation that uses the community tier for ten years and never pays us a euro is doing exactly what we built the community tier to enable.

## What this implies for pricing

We have not published a pricing sheet, and we are not going to do so in this post. The pricing for each enterprise tier is set in customer conversations because the right number depends on the customer's deployment scale, the curation cadence they need, the integration depth they require, and the support level they want. Publishing a single number for the CISO tier, the DPO tier, and the CFO tier would be a marketing convenience that does not match how those subscriptions actually get sized.

What we will say in the abstract is that the pricing is anchored to the **value the persona is buying**, not to the **cost of the bits**. The DPO who is buying the multilingual NER bundle is buying the avoided cost of the in-house ML team they would otherwise need to maintain those models, plus the avoided regulatory risk of the months of delay before the in-house team gets the models production-ready. The CISO who is buying the injection corpus is buying the avoided cost of the threat-intelligence team they would otherwise need to keep the corpus current, plus the avoided incident risk of running with a corpus that goes stale. The CFO who is buying the chargeback integration is buying the avoided cost of the finance-engineering team that would otherwise need to build the integration, plus the avoided friction of running AI cost attribution outside the existing financial systems.

The community tier is, by design, free of these pricing pressures. The community tier exists because we believe gateway-level governance is structurally necessary for any organisation routing meaningful traffic to LLM providers, and the community tier is what makes that necessary capability accessible to everyone — including organisations whose buyer-persona profile does not match any of the enterprise tiers, and including organisations who are simply not at the scale where enterprise-tier curation would be cost-justified. The community tier is not a loss-leader; it is the load-bearing floor of what the product is.

## What we expect over time

We expect the community tier to keep gaining capabilities at the same rate the engineering team ships them. The v0.2 release in flight (the `ariactl` CLI, reversible AES-256-backed tokenisation, the JaCoCo coverage gate, the Karar B token role-overhead semantics) is entirely Apache 2.0. So is the v0.3 work that supports the enterprise tiers — the engine code for vector-similarity injection scoring, the model-loading infrastructure for clinical NER variants, the billing-event format that the chargeback integration consumes. The split between community and enterprise tier is at the *curation* boundary, not at the *capability* boundary; the community tier gets every capability that does not require sustained external curation.

We expect the enterprise tiers to take longer to mature than the community tier did, because the curation work is genuinely harder than the engineering work. Building an injection corpus that catches novel attack variants reliably is a multi-year investment with a regular update cycle, not a one-time shipping milestone. The first enterprise-tier deployments are likely to be heavily-customised for specific customers and to evolve into a more standardised offering as the curated assets become production-grade. We have no illusions about how long this takes; we are budgeting for it accordingly.

We expect the community tier to continue to be the product most operators run, indefinitely. This is a feature of the model, not a bug. The enterprise tiers exist for the operators whose specific persona-driven needs justify them; the community tier exists for everyone else, and we expect "everyone else" to be the bulk of the user base for as long as the product exists.

## Closing

The reason we wrote this post is that the open-core licensing question keeps coming up in conversations about Gatekeeper, and the answer "we did open core but persona-gated" tends to elicit follow-up questions that take longer to answer than a conversation usefully accommodates. The longer answer is in this post. The short version is that we believe gateway-level AI governance is a category that is best served by a complete community-tier product and persona-specific enterprise tiers that add to that product rather than gating away from it. The economics make sense, the product structure makes sense, and the alignment with how governance is actually bought in customer organisations makes sense.
