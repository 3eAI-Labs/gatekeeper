# 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper for Public Sector

The government agency that decides to put a generative-AI assistant in front of a citizen-facing service is making a decision under unusually direct accountability. Citizens have legal rights about how their personal data is processed, parliamentary committees ask hard questions about which third parties see government data, and procurement frameworks require evidence of controls long before deployment is approved. The same pressures apply to internal-facing public-sector AI deployments — assistants that help case officers process applications, summarise correspondence, or draft response letters — because the data those assistants see is exactly the data the agency has the strictest custodial duty over.

This brief explains how 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper addresses AI governance in a public-sector context, with attention to data sovereignty, on-premises deployment, and regulatory frameworks that span KVKK, GDPR, and PDPL across the markets where 3eAI Labs operates.

## What public-sector readers recognise

Three things are reliably true in public-sector AI evaluations.

The data sovereignty story is non-negotiable. Many government agencies operate under explicit regulatory or policy requirements that personal data of citizens must remain in-country, or at minimum within a defined regional boundary. This rules out a substantial portion of the LLM provider market for in-line use; the agencies that have moved forward with AI deployments have typically done so with either self-hosted open-weights models (Llama variants, Qwen variants, Mistral variants) running on infrastructure they control, or with regional cloud-provider offerings (Azure OpenAI in specific regions, regional API gateways) that contractually keep data in-region. A governance product that does not work with these self-hosted or regional patterns is not a public-sector option.

The audit and accountability story is genuinely strict. Parliamentary oversight, freedom-of-information requests, ombudsman investigations, and ministerial accountability all create a continuous requirement for the agency to be able to answer "what did the AI assistant see, when, and on whose behalf" for any citizen interaction it touched. This is not a theoretical audit — it is an operational reality. A governance product that stores audit events in a non-portable format, or in a cloud the agency does not control, or with retention shorter than the agency's records-management requirements, is creating an audit gap rather than closing one.

The procurement framework story is structural. Public-sector procurement typically requires evidence of security controls, proof of architectural choices, vendor-disclosure documentation, and code-review-grade transparency before deployment is approved. Open-source products with full source visibility, an active issue tracker, documented architecture decisions, and an honest accounting of known gaps tend to fit these procurement frameworks much better than closed-source products that require extensive vendor-disclosure paperwork to evaluate.

## What Gatekeeper does for a public-sector AI integration

The three Gatekeeper modules each address a public-sector-specific concern.

**`aria-shield` provides the cost discipline and provider-routing flexibility that public-sector deployments need.** Per-team token quotas and per-model dollar budgets fit the cost-attribution requirements of public-sector budget management — different departments, different programs, different ministerial portfolios all get their own quota with their own attribution. Multi-provider routing is particularly valuable in public-sector contexts because it means the same governance posture applies whether the actual upstream is an internally-hosted Llama variant on government infrastructure (for the most sensitive workloads), a regional Azure OpenAI deployment (for moderately sensitive workloads), or a general-purpose external provider (for citizen-facing public-information workloads where the data sensitivity is lower). The circuit breaker means an outage at any one provider does not take down the AI-assisted services that citizens or staff have come to rely on; the threshold alerts mean budget conversations happen before fiscal-year boundaries are crossed.

**`aria-mask` provides the data-protection discipline that public-sector citizen-data handling requires.** The eight built-in PII patterns include several that matter directly for public-sector deployments: TC Kimlik (Turkish national ID, with mod-11 checksum) for Turkish-jurisdiction agencies, MSISDN for citizen contact details, IBAN for benefit-payment flows, email for citizen-correspondence flows, and date-of-birth as a structural pattern. The role-based policy resolution distinguishes the case-officer role (which sees the relevant fields for the application being processed) from the supervisor role (which sees broader context) from the external-partner role (which sees the minimum required for inter-agency cooperation). For PII that does not match a structural pattern — citizen names in correspondence, free-text addresses, descriptions of life circumstances in benefit applications — the NER pipeline runs Apache OpenNLP for English and the multilingual Turkish-BERT model by default for Turkish content. The pluggable engine architecture means agencies operating in additional languages (Arabic for MENA-region deployments, Persian, Russian, French, German for various European deployments) can configure the appropriate engines for their citizen population.

**`aria-canary` provides the change-safety discipline that public-sector deployments require.** Government agencies are particularly conservative about changes to citizen-facing services for good reason — a regression in a benefits-application assistant is a citizen-impact event, not just a metrics change. Multi-stage progressive splitting with consistent-hashed routing means citizens using the service during a rollout see consistent behaviour through their interactions; auto-rollback on error-rate sustain means a regression rolls itself back without requiring the duty officer to manually intervene; traffic shadowing with structural diff between baseline and candidate model responses lets the responsible delivery team see exactly how a candidate model differs from production on actual workload, before any citizen sees the candidate's output. The diff is captured as durable evidence — operators can show parliamentary or audit committees exactly what changed and how, which is the standard of evidence public-sector accountability frameworks expect.

## On-premises deployment is the default

The deployment topology that fits a typical public-sector deployment is **fully self-hosted**. Gatekeeper is designed to run on infrastructure the agency controls: on-premises Kubernetes clusters, government-cloud Kubernetes (G-Cloud, KamuNet equivalents in various jurisdictions), or single-host VMs in air-gapped environments. The [DEPLOYMENT guide](../../../runtime/docs/DEPLOYMENT.md) documents the Kubernetes sidecar topology, the single-host topology, and the docker-compose topology in production-ready detail.

The data stores follow the same pattern. Redis Cluster runs on agency-controlled infrastructure (or a single Redis instance for smaller deployments); PostgreSQL runs on agency-controlled infrastructure with whatever encryption-at-rest and access controls the agency's standard operating environment requires. No cloud-managed dependencies are required; no telemetry phones home; no licence-server check-in is needed for the community tier (the Apache 2.0 community tier is a complete product and operates indefinitely without external dependencies).

For agencies running upstream LLM providers self-hosted — Llama or Qwen variants on their own GPU infrastructure — the multi-provider routing handles those endpoints the same way it handles cloud providers. The provider transformation logic in `aria-provider.lua` covers OpenAI-compatible endpoints natively, and self-hosted Llama / Qwen / Mistral deployments via vLLM, llama.cpp server, or Ollama all expose OpenAI-compatible interfaces. The agency keeps full control of the model weights, the inference infrastructure, and the data flow; Gatekeeper just provides the governance layer in front of it.

## Compliance posture in public-sector contexts

Throughout this brief — and throughout all our documentation — we describe what Gatekeeper does in support of operator audits in regulatory contexts. We do not certify compliance with any framework. Public-sector organisations operate under audit boundaries that span constitutional rights, primary legislation, secondary regulations, and operational policy; Gatekeeper is one of many controls that contribute to the agency's compliance posture.

The relevant capabilities by framework:

For **GDPR (EU agencies and EU-citizen data handled by non-EU agencies)**, the product supports data minimisation through PII masking at the gateway edge, purpose limitation through role-based policies, and the durable seven-year audit trail that supports the access-logging requirements that data-protection regulators expect. The data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure) operate at a higher level than the AI traffic; Gatekeeper supports those rights indirectly by ensuring the AI traffic does not become a secondary repository of personal data outside the agency's primary records system.

For **KVKK (Turkish public-sector deployments)**, the same masking and audit primitives apply, with the additional capability of the TC Kimlik mod-11 checksum validation for the Turkish national ID and the default Turkish-BERT NER model for free-text Turkish content. Article 12 of KVKK requires data controllers to take appropriate technical measures to ensure data security; Gatekeeper's role-based policies, audit trail, and PII masking are technical measures that contribute to that requirement.

For **PDPL (MENA public-sector deployments — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, others)**, the geographic-policy enforcement through consumer metadata supports data-localisation requirements: routing decisions can be conditioned on the consumer's geography to keep data in-region, and the multi-provider routing supports keeping the most sensitive workloads on regional cloud or self-hosted infrastructure.

For **national security review and procurement frameworks**, the open-source nature of the community tier means agency security reviews can read every line of code that handles citizen data — there is no vendor-confidential blob to take on faith. The architecture decision register documents every non-trivial design choice with explicit rationale; the release notes enumerate every known limitation by name; the source code is on GitHub under Apache 2.0. Procurement teams that have struggled to evaluate closed-source AI-governance products tend to find this transparency directly useful.

What Gatekeeper does **not** do, and what no public-sector agency should expect from it: it does not provide cryptographic-grade tokenisation or pseudonymisation to the standard required for some research-data-sharing frameworks (those require purpose-built toolchains), it does not certify the agency's overall compliance posture (which requires controls across the entire system), and it does not replace the agency's existing access-control or records-management infrastructure (it covers AI traffic specifically, not the full data layer).

## Operational transparency

The operational story for public-sector deployments emphasises predictability and auditability. The Helm chart ships with a `PrometheusRule` template that wires into the agency's existing Prometheus stack, three Grafana dashboards (Shield, Mask, Canary) that auto-discover via the standard sidecar-injection annotations, and structured JSON logging throughout — no proprietary log formats, no agent-required telemetry, just the standard observability stack the agency already operates.

The audit pipeline lands events in PostgreSQL with the standard append-only design — `DO INSTEAD NOTHING` rules inside PostgreSQL prevent any UPDATE or DELETE on the audit tables, which makes the audit trail tamper-proof even from a privileged database administrator. The audit events carry consumer ID, route ID, action taken, masking rule that fired, and a metadata excerpt — never the original PII values — which means the audit trail itself respects the data-minimisation principle that drove its creation. The retention is configurable; the seven-year default suits most jurisdictions but agencies operating under longer retention windows (twenty years for certain regulatory contexts) can configure accordingly.

For agencies integrating with existing audit aggregation infrastructure, the audit table can be replicated to downstream systems through standard PostgreSQL replication or change-data-capture tooling. Gatekeeper does not assume its audit table is the only audit destination; agencies that already operate centralised audit aggregation can include Gatekeeper events in that aggregation through their existing pipeline.

## Honest about what's not in v0.1.1

Four minor gaps are worth knowing about for public-sector evaluations:

The `ariactl` CLI promised in our original architecture is deferred to v0.2; in v0.1.1 the canary control endpoints are exposed through the APISIX plugin control plane and operators script against them with `curl`. The sidecar's deeper prompt-side security capabilities (`PromptAnalyzer` for vector-similarity injection scoring, `ContentFilter` for output moderation) are stubs in v0.1.1 — these are part of the v0.3 enterprise CISO tier; the v0.1.1 community tier covers prompt-injection through a regex-based scan, which catches known patterns but is not a complete defence against novel injection variants. Reversible tokenisation backed by AES-256 is deferred to v0.2 — the v0.1 `tokenize` strategy emits a non-reversible hash. The Karar B token role-overhead semantics for multi-message chat-completion requests is open as a v0.2 ADR — total token counts may be off by 1-3 tokens per role-overhead boundary, immaterial for budget enforcement but worth knowing about for finance-grade reconciliation.

A public-sector agency evaluating Gatekeeper should expect the v0.1.1 release to satisfy general PII masking, cost control, and progressive-delivery use cases for AI workloads, with the four gaps above documented as known limitations and roadmapped to specific subsequent releases. The architecture decision register and release notes provide the level of transparency that procurement evaluations typically require.
