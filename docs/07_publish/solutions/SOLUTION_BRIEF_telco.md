# 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper for Telecommunications

The conversations a telco has about AI in 2026 are not the same conversations any other industry has. The customer-care chatbot that summarises a billing dispute is also looking at the customer's MSISDN, their IMEI, the home address attached to the account, and three months of call records. The network-operations assistant that helps a junior engineer triage an alarm is being shown SS7 trace fragments, BSS records, and topology data that the carrier is contractually forbidden from letting leave the operator's environment. The self-service portal that lets a subscriber check their data balance is, behind the scenes, exposing the same APIs that power the agent-facing systems — and the LLM the operator has bolted onto that portal sees them all.

This brief explains how 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper addresses the AI governance problem in a telco context specifically, without pretending it is a TMF-certified product or claiming compliance it cannot back up.

## Why telcos read this brief differently

Three things are true in a telco that are not true in most other industries. First, the regulatory environment around customer data is genuinely strict and genuinely audited — KVKK in Turkey, GDPR in EU operations, PDPL in MENA markets, BSI baseline controls in DE, ANATEL data protection in Brazil, the list goes on. AI vendors who treat compliance as a marketing checkbox do not survive the first audit cycle. Second, the data classes that matter are sector-specific in ways that generic PII detection misses — MSISDN with country-code formatting, IMEI with Luhn checksum, IMSI with operator-prefix lookup, ICCID, the home-zone CGI, the cell-tower BTS identifier — none of these are in any commodity NER model's training set. Third, the operator's existing infrastructure is mature: there is already a gateway (often APISIX, often Kong, often a custom layer in front of TMF Open APIs), there is already a Prometheus stack, there is already a 24x7 NOC. New AI-governance infrastructure that does not slot into this is dead on arrival.

## What Gatekeeper does for a telco AI integration

The three Gatekeeper modules map directly onto the three conversations telco operators are having about AI right now.

**`aria-shield` is the cost and routing layer for AI calls inside the operator.** Per-team token quotas and per-model dollar budgets give the FinOps function the chargeback they need to attribute LLM spend to the right business line — customer-care assistants, NOC tooling, marketing automation, legal-discovery summaries — instead of having a single line item that nobody owns. Multi-provider routing with circuit-breaker failover (OpenAI, Anthropic, Azure OpenAI, Gemini, plus self-hosted Llama / Qwen variants for data-residency-sensitive workloads) means the carrier is not locked into a single provider and can shift traffic between them on policy or cost grounds. Threshold alerts to webhooks and Slack mean the budget conversation happens before the bill arrives, not after.

**`aria-mask` is the data-egress layer for telco-specific PII.** The eight built-in PII regex patterns include MSISDN (with country-code awareness), TC Kimlik (with mod-11 checksum, particularly relevant for Turkey-based operators), IBAN (with ISO-7064 checksum, for billing flows), IMEI (with Luhn checksum, for device-management flows), and email — covering most of the structural PII a telco support assistant or self-service backend would naturally encounter. The twelve mask strategies range from `last4` (keep the last four digits, useful for support agents who need to verify a customer is reading the right number back) through to `redact` (replace entirely with a placeholder, for downstream analytics or LLM prompts where the full value is not needed). The role-based policy resolution — administrator, support agent, external partner, unknown — maps cleanly onto the access-control models telcos already use. For data that does not match a structural pattern (a customer's name in free-text, a street address that the regex did not anticipate), the NER bridge pipeline runs Apache OpenNLP for English content and a multilingual Turkish-BERT model by default for Turkish content, with the engine pluggable for Arabic, Persian, Russian, or anything else the carrier needs.

**`aria-canary` is the change-safety layer for swapping AI models or upstream providers.** Telcos are particularly conservative about model changes for good reason — a regression in a customer-care assistant is a customer-experience incident, not a backend metric. Multi-stage progressive splitting (5% → 10% → 25% → 50% → 100%) with consistent-hashed routing means that the same customer keeps hitting the same assistant version through the experiment, which prevents the "this customer just got a worse answer than they did yesterday" complaints that erode trust in AI features. Auto-rollback on error-rate sustain means that a regression visible in metrics rolls itself back without an SRE having to be paged. Traffic shadowing with structural diff between baseline and candidate responses lets the operator see exactly how the new model differs from the old one before any live customer sees the new model's output — a pre-deployment safety check that is hard to do any other way.

## Carrier-grade operational story

The deployment shape that fits a typical carrier is the **Kubernetes sidecar topology**: APISIX and the Aria runtime sidecar in the same Pod, the Aria runtime listening on `127.0.0.1:8081` only (no external bind, NetworkPolicy in the Helm chart restricts ingress to the same Pod as defence-in-depth), Redis Cluster for real-time state, PostgreSQL for the durable audit trail. The Helm chart ships with a `PrometheusRule` template wired into the carrier's existing Prometheus stack, three Grafana dashboards (Shield, Mask, Canary), and a NetworkPolicy template that operators tune to their specific environment.

The audit trail lands in PostgreSQL with monthly partitioning and a seven-year retention default — long enough for almost every regulatory audit window in the markets telcos operate in. The append-only design (PostgreSQL `DO INSTEAD NOTHING` rules on UPDATE and DELETE) means the audit trail is tamper-proof from inside the database itself; even a privileged operator cannot retroactively edit an audit record. The audit pipeline drains every five seconds (configurable via `aria.audit.flush-interval-ms`) from the Redis buffer to the PostgreSQL table; the design (ADR-009) is an LPOP polling loop rather than an HTTP bridge specifically because audit is async-by-design and the buffer is the natural decoupling point between the request hot path and the durable persistence.

## Compliance posture in telco contexts

Throughout this brief — and throughout all our documentation — we describe what Gatekeeper *does* in support of operator audits in regulatory contexts. We do not certify compliance with any framework. Telco operators operate within audit boundaries that are far broader than what a gateway plugin can claim to address; Gatekeeper is one of many controls that operators use to satisfy those boundaries.

The relevant capabilities by framework:

For **KVKK (Turkey)**, the product supports Turkish-specific PII detection (TC Kimlik with mod-11 checksum), the default Turkish-BERT NER model for free-text personal-data detection, role-based masking policies that map onto KVKK's data-minimisation principle, and the durable seven-year audit trail that supports KVKK Article 12 retention requirements. For **GDPR (EU operations)**, the same masking and audit primitives apply, with the addition of the per-consumer data-minimisation framing that GDPR's purpose-limitation principle requires. For **PDPL (MENA operations — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, others)**, geographic-policy enforcement through consumer metadata supports data-localisation requirements: routing decisions can be conditioned on the consumer's geography to keep data in-region. For **TMF Open API alignment**, Gatekeeper sits in front of TMF endpoints rather than reimplementing them — the gateway plugin model means TMF endpoint owners do not need to embed governance code, and Gatekeeper inherits the routing and authentication that TMF deployments already do at the gateway layer.

What Gatekeeper does **not** do, and what no operator should expect from it: it does not certify TMF-compliant behaviour of upstream APIs (that is the upstream API's own responsibility), it does not satisfy carrier-grade lawful-intercept requirements (which require purpose-built infrastructure outside the AI traffic pattern), and it does not claim to be a complete data-protection control plane for the whole carrier (it covers AI traffic only).

## Honest about what's not in v0.1.1

Four minor gaps are documented in our release notes and worth knowing about for telco evaluations:

The `ariactl` CLI promised in our original architecture is deferred to v0.2; in v0.1.1 the canary control endpoints are exposed through the APISIX plugin control plane and operators script against them with `curl` (which is what production users do today). The sidecar's `PromptAnalyzer` and `ContentFilter` deep implementations are stubs in v0.1.1 — vector-similarity prompt-injection scoring and content moderation are part of our v0.3 enterprise CISO tier, and the Lua-side regex prompt-injection scan covers community-tier needs in the meantime. Reversible tokenisation backed by AES-256 is deferred to v0.2; the v0.1 `tokenize` mask strategy emits a non-reversible hash. The Karar B token role-overhead semantics for multi-message chat-completion requests is open as a v0.2 ADR — operators see total token counts that may be off by 1-3 tokens per role-overhead boundary, which is not material for billing reconciliation but is worth knowing about.

None of these gaps prevent a telco from running Gatekeeper in production today. They are documented because we believe operators deserve to know what is and is not in the box before they deploy.
