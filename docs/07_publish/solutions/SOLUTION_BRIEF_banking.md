# 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper for Banking & Financial Services

The bank that puts a generative-AI assistant in front of a customer service flow makes a particular kind of bet. The bet is that the assistant will be good enough at routine queries — balance checks, dispute initiation, transaction explanations — to relieve the contact-centre load, while remaining safe enough that no customer's account number ends up in a model provider's training data and no privileged action gets triggered by a cleverly-crafted prompt. The bet is structurally sound — banks have been routing more sophisticated workloads through more constrained gateways for thirty years — but it requires the same kind of operational discipline that the rest of the bank's customer-facing infrastructure already has.

This brief explains how 3e-Aria-Gatekeeper addresses AI governance in a banking context, with attention to the regulatory specificities that banks operate under and an honest accounting of what the v0.1.1 release does and does not do.

## What banks recognise immediately

Three things tend to ring true for banking infrastructure leaders looking at AI governance.

The cost story matters more than the marketing decks acknowledge. A retail bank with twelve million customers running an AI-assisted contact-centre flow at 5% adoption is making roughly 600,000 LLM API calls per day — at $0.01 per call (a deliberately conservative estimate that includes both the input and output tokens of a typical short interaction), that is roughly $2.2M per year just for one channel. When the same bank adds a second channel (mobile self-service), and a third (in-app proactive assistance), and a fourth (agent copilot), the LLM line item is in tens of millions of dollars annually with no per-line-of-business attribution unless it is built in from the start.

The data-egress story is more nuanced than "block PAN at the perimeter". Banks operate under PCI-DSS scope hygiene that requires cardholder data to never leave the audited cardholder-data environment without going through specific controls. An LLM provider is, by definition, outside that audited environment, which makes the gateway between the bank's applications and the LLM provider one of the controls. But it is not the only control, and it does not certify PCI-DSS compliance — the audit boundary remains the bank's responsibility, and any vendor who claims their gateway plugin "makes you PCI compliant" is either misunderstanding the certification process or being deliberately misleading.

The audit story is the one banks already have strong opinions about. The bank's existing audit pipeline is the one regulators have already accepted, and the bank does not want a new audit silo for AI traffic — it wants AI traffic events to land in the same audit infrastructure (PostgreSQL, S3, Splunk, whatever the bank's choice is) that already serves the rest of the regulatory load. A governance product that ships its own non-portable audit format is making the bank's life harder, not easier.

## What Gatekeeper does for a banking AI integration

The three Gatekeeper modules each address a specific banking concern.

**`aria-shield` provides the cost-control discipline that AI workloads need to fit into a bank's FinOps practice.** Per-team token quotas and per-model dollar budgets create the per-business-line attribution that lets the bank treat AI spend the way it treats any other infrastructure spend — with named owners, defensible allocations, and a chargeback model that scales. Multi-provider routing with circuit-breaker failover means the bank is not locked into a single provider, which matters both for cost arbitrage (different providers have different pricing curves) and for resilience (a single provider's outage does not take down the AI-assisted channels). Threshold alerts at 80%, 90%, and 100% of quota mean the conversation about budget happens in time to do something about it, not after the bill has already arrived.

**`aria-mask` provides the cardholder-data scope-hygiene that PCI-DSS audits expect to see at the AI traffic boundary.** The PAN regex pattern with Luhn validation catches structurally-valid card numbers in prompts and applies the configured mask strategy — `redact` by default for cardholder data, with `last4` available for the support-agent flows where the agent legitimately needs to verify a customer is reading the right number. The IBAN regex with ISO-7064 validation catches account numbers, particularly important for European operations and for SEPA transactions. The role-based policy resolution (administrator, support agent, external partner, unknown) maps naturally onto a bank's access control model — different roles see different mask intensities of the same response. For PII that does not match a structural pattern (a customer's name in free-text, an address that the regex did not anticipate), the NER pipeline runs Apache OpenNLP for English content and the Turkish-BERT model by default for Turkish content; banks operating in multiple jurisdictions can configure additional engines as needed.

**`aria-canary` provides the change-safety discipline that bank infrastructure leaders already require for everything else they ship.** Multi-stage progressive splitting with consistent-hashed routing means model upgrades roll out under controlled conditions; auto-rollback on error-rate sustain means that a regression in an assistant flow rolls itself back without requiring manual intervention; traffic shadowing with structural diff between baseline and candidate responses lets risk-and-controls teams see exactly how a candidate model differs from the production model before any live customer sees the candidate's output. This last capability is particularly valuable for banks because it converts model-upgrade decisions from "trust the vendor's benchmark numbers" into "see the actual response delta on our actual production traffic", which is the standard the bank's existing change-control board already applies to other infrastructure changes.

## Compliance posture in banking contexts

Throughout this brief — and throughout all our documentation — we describe what Gatekeeper does in support of operator audits in regulatory contexts. We do not certify compliance with any framework. Banks operate within audit boundaries that are far broader than what a gateway plugin can claim to address; Gatekeeper is one of many controls.

The relevant capabilities by framework:

For **PCI-DSS scope hygiene**, the product detects PAN-shaped strings in prompts (Luhn-validated) and applies mask or block strategies to prevent cardholder data from egressing to upstream LLM providers. This is a *capability statement*: the product **does not claim PCI-DSS compliance**. PCI-DSS compliance requires an audited cardholder-data environment, which remains the bank's audit boundary, and is satisfied through a combination of network controls, access controls, monitoring, and process controls of which Gatekeeper is one part. Any audit narrative the bank produces should describe Gatekeeper as a control that supports the cardholder-data egress prevention requirement, not as a control that certifies PCI-DSS compliance.

For **GDPR / KVKK / PDPL** (depending on the bank's operating jurisdictions), the product supports data-minimisation through PII masking at the gateway edge before personal data reaches third-party LLM providers, and supports purpose-limitation through role-based masking policies that distinguish access by user role. The audit trail in PostgreSQL — append-only, partitioned monthly with seven-year retention — supports the retention requirements of all three frameworks.

For **internal audit and regulatory reporting**, the audit pipeline lands events in PostgreSQL in a tabular format that integrates straightforwardly with the bank's existing data warehouse and reporting infrastructure. The events carry the consumer ID, route ID, action taken, masking rule that fired, and a metadata excerpt — never the original PII values — which gives the audit team enough to reconstruct what happened on a given request without retaining the actual personal data the request contained.

What Gatekeeper does **not** do, and what no bank should expect from it: it does not satisfy SOX requirements for financial-reporting controls (different audit boundary entirely), it does not provide the cardholder-data tokenisation that some PCI-DSS implementations require (the v0.1 `tokenize` strategy is non-reversible — reversible AES-256-backed tokenisation is deferred to v0.2), and it does not replace the bank's existing fraud-detection or AML controls (which operate on structured transactions, not on AI prompt content).

## Operational shape that fits a bank

The deployment topology that fits a typical bank is the **Kubernetes sidecar topology** documented in the [DEPLOYMENT guide](../../../runtime/docs/DEPLOYMENT.md): APISIX and the Aria runtime sidecar in the same Pod, the sidecar binding only to `127.0.0.1:8081` (no external network exposure), Redis Cluster for real-time state, PostgreSQL for the durable audit trail, and a NetworkPolicy template restricting ingress to the same Pod as defence-in-depth. The Helm chart ships with a `PrometheusRule` template that wires into the bank's existing Prometheus stack, three Grafana dashboards (Shield, Mask, Canary) that auto-discover via the standard sidecar-injection annotations, and a Helm migration Job for deployments where the sidecar's database role lacks DDL grants and the schema bootstrap needs to run separately.

For banks with stricter network controls — locked-down Pods, no internal HTTP between containers, audited DDL grants — the configuration knobs all exist: `ARIA_FLYWAY_ENABLED=false` disables the sidecar's startup migration and lets the Helm migration Job (or an external migration tool) own the schema; the sidecar's HTTP bridge can be replaced with the gRPC services that exist for forward-compat (though this requires a non-Lua client and is not the documented happy path); and the audit pipeline's flush interval is tunable from 250ms (for sub-second visibility) to several minutes (for low-overhead deployments).

Operational observability follows banking conventions. All emissions are structured JSON logs with consistent field names, the PrometheusRule template ships with starter alerts (`aria_canary_rollback_fired`, `aria_quota_breach_threshold`, `aria_audit_failed_drain_rate`) that the bank's existing on-call rotation can pick up directly, and the audit table schema is documented in [`DB_SCHEMA.md`](../../04_design/DB_SCHEMA.md) for downstream data-warehouse integration.

## Honest about what's not in v0.1.1

Four minor gaps are worth knowing about for banking evaluations:

The `ariactl` CLI promised in our original architecture is deferred to v0.2; in v0.1.1 the canary control endpoints are exposed through the APISIX plugin control plane and operators script against them with `curl` — which is what current production users do, but is not the same operator experience a bank's DevOps team would expect long-term. The sidecar's deeper prompt-side security capabilities (`PromptAnalyzer` for vector-similarity injection scoring, `ContentFilter` for output moderation) are stubs in v0.1.1 — these are part of the v0.3 enterprise CISO tier where the value justifies the curated injection corpus and content-moderation policy that backs them; the v0.1.1 community tier covers prompt-injection through a regex-based scan, which catches known patterns but not novel vector-similar variations. Reversible tokenisation backed by AES-256 is deferred to v0.2 — the v0.1 `tokenize` strategy emits a non-reversible hash, which is sufficient for opaque-token audit-trail use cases but not sufficient for cardholder-data tokenisation flows that require detokenisation later. The Karar B token role-overhead semantics for multi-message chat-completion requests is open as a v0.2 ADR — total token counts may be off by 1-3 tokens per role-overhead boundary, immaterial for budget enforcement but worth knowing about for finance-grade reconciliation.

A bank evaluating Gatekeeper should expect the v0.1.1 release to satisfy the cost-control, PII-masking, and progressive-delivery use cases for general AI traffic, with the four gaps above documented as known limitations. For deployments where reversible cardholder-data tokenisation is a hard requirement, v0.2 is the appropriate target. For deployments where vector-similarity prompt-injection detection is a hard requirement, the v0.3 enterprise CISO tier is the appropriate target.
